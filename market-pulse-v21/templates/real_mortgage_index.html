How expensive is it really to own a home?

What does the number mean?

Imagine the same buyer in January 1990 vs. today. They earn the same paycheck (in today's dollars). They want the same kind of home. Could they afford it?

That's what this index answers. 100 is the cost of owning a home in January 1990. 150 means today's buyer's monthly mortgage payment buys 50% less house, or costs 50% more, in real terms — even after adjusting for inflation, even after the actual rate they'd pay today. 75 would mean the opposite: housing is 25% cheaper than 1990.

Why this is more honest than "home prices"

Headlines love the median sale price. But that number ignores two huge things: interest rates and inflation.

A house at 3% interest with 1990s inflation is a totally different financial reality from the same house at 7% with 2020s inflation. List prices hide what you'd actually pay each month — which is what people actually feel.

Example: A $400,000 home at 3% costs ~$1,500/mo (P&I, 10% down). The same home at 7% costs ~$2,400/mo. Same price tag, 60% more expensive to live in.

What 2006 vs. today looks like

In 2006, the housing bubble peaked. Prices were sky-high but rates sat around 6%. Most buyers knew it was getting silly.

Today's reading is often higher than 2006 — not because prices are wildly higher (they are, but only somewhat), but because rates went from ~3% in 2021 to ~7% by late 2022. Both factors moved against buyers at once. That's what the chart is showing you.

That's the punchline. Adjusted for inflation and the rate you'd actually pay, housing has rarely been less affordable in the modern era.

How this is calculated

  1. Take the typical home's sale price each month — using the S&P CoreLogic Case-Shiller Home Price Index, the most-cited measure of U.S. home prices.
  2. Calculate the actual monthly mortgage payment at that month's real interest rate (Freddie Mac's 30-year fixed rate, the standard benchmark), assuming the chosen down payment and a 30-year loan.
  3. Adjust for inflation since January 1990 using the CPI for "all items less shelter." Why exclude shelter? Because home prices are the thing we're measuring — deflating by an index that includes shelter would mute the very effect we're trying to see.
  4. Compare to the same calculation for January 1990 and express it as a percentage. So 148 means owning costs 48% more per month than it did in January 1990, in real, inflation-adjusted, rate-adjusted terms.
index[t] = (monthly_payment[t] / monthly_payment[Jan 1990]) × (cpi[Jan 1990] / cpi[t]) × 100

Sources: Case-Shiller Home Price Index (S&P/CoreLogic, via FRED) · Freddie Mac 30-year fixed mortgage rate · BLS CPI-U All Items Less Shelter · Methodology adapted from John Wake, RealEstateDecoded.com. Updated daily from FRED.
