How does this work?

What's the home actually worth at today's mortgage rates? Take a baseline price + payment from a year when mortgages were normal (we use ~5 years ago, when the 30-year fixed was ≈3%), inflate the payment by cumulative CPI to today, then ask: what home price produces that inflated payment at today's much higher rate? That's "fair value."

  1. Baseline — state's median home value 5 years ago.
  2. PITI then — standard 20%-down PITI at the 2021-ish mortgage rate, using state property tax + insurance.
  3. Inflate — multiply the payment by today's CPI ÷ 2021's CPI. A $1,000 payment in 2021 had the same buying-power claim on a household budget as ~$1,170 today (16-17% cumulative).
  4. Back-solve — given today's mortgage rate, what home price produces the inflated payment? The math is a one-line linear solve once you have state tax + insurance constants.
  5. Compare — current state median ÷ fair value. >0% = market is asking more than payment-equivalence justifies (overvalued). <0% = bargain (undervalued).

Caveats: assumes 20% down + 30Y fixed (FHA/VA buyers face different math). State-level medians smooth over wild ZIP variance — a state at 60% overvalued has cheap ZIPs and expensive ones, but the median is what the median is. CPI + baseline mortgage rate from FRED, cached 24h.
